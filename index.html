The Laws That Govern Motion

Newton's three laws of motion form the foundation of classical mechanics, explaining the relationship between a body and the forces acting upon it.

First Law: Law of Inertia

An object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.

Example: When a car stops suddenly, passengers continue moving forward (inertia) until the seatbelt applies a force to stop them.

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

F = m × a

Example: It takes more force to accelerate a loaded truck than an empty one because the loaded truck has greater mass.

Third Law: Action-Reaction

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Example: When you push against a wall, the wall pushes back with equal force. Rockets work by expelling gas downward, which pushes the rocket upward.

Universal Gravitation

Newton's law of universal gravitation revolutionized astronomy by providing a mathematical explanation for planetary motion.

"Gravity explains the motions of the planets, but it cannot explain who set the planets in motion."
— Isaac Newton

Newton proposed that every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them:

F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r²

Where:

  • F is the gravitational force between two objects
  • G is the gravitational constant (6.674×10⁻¹¹ N·(m/kg)²)
  • m₁ and m₂ are the masses of the objects
  • r is the distance between their centers

Revolutionary Impact

Newton's discoveries fundamentally changed how we understand the physical world and laid the foundation for modern physics and engineering.

Key Contributions:

  • Celestial Mechanics: Explained planetary motion and cometary orbits
  • Tides: Showed how the Moon's gravity creates ocean tides
  • Engineering: Enabled calculation of forces in structures and machines
  • Space Exploration: Essential for calculating rocket trajectories and satellite orbits
  • Mathematics: Developed calculus (simultaneously with Leibniz) to solve physics problems

    • Gravitational forces between Earth and Moon

Biography

Isaac Newton (1643–1727) was an English mathematician, physicist, astronomer, alchemist, theologian, and author who is widely recognized as one of the greatest mathematicians and most influential scientists of all time.

1643

Born prematurely on January 4 in Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire, England. His father had died three months earlier.

1661

Entered Trinity College, Cambridge, where he studied Aristotle's work but became fascinated by modern philosophers like Descartes.

1665-1666

"Annus mirabilis" (miracle year) when Cambridge closed due to plague. Developed theories of calculus, optics, and gravitation.

1687

Published Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica, establishing classical mechanics.

1703

Became President of the Royal Society, a position he held until his death.

1727

Died in London on March 31 and was buried in Westminster Abbey with great honors.

Enduring Legacy

Newton's work continues to influence science and technology centuries after his death.

Modern Applications:

  • Space Exploration: NASA and other space agencies use Newton's laws for spacecraft navigation
  • Engineering: Essential for designing vehicles, buildings, and machines
  • Physics Education: Still the foundation of introductory physics courses worldwide
